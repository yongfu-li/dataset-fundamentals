Transcript

Slide 1 — Chapter 3 — Utility, dilemmas, and consequences

Many projects create value only by using personal information—which creates standing tension between utility and privacy. This part covers that trade-off, dilemmas when principles conflict, and the individual and organizational harms that follow when ethics is neglected.

Slide 2 — Learning objectives

By the end of this part, you should explain the utility–privacy tension, describe two ethical dilemma patterns, and list consequences of ignoring ethics for people and organizations.

Slide 3 — Balancing utility and privacy

Personalized care, safer transport, or better public services can justify collection—yet the same pipelines can enable surveillance, exclusion, or secondary uses subjects never expected. Teams assess benefits and residual risks, prefer less identifying representations when analysis still works, and treat statutes as floors rather than ceilings. The ethical task is deciding whether proposed utility warrants the privacy burden at all.

Slide 4 — Example 3.7 — Utility–privacy tension in personal predictions

Example 3.7 states the trade-off in prediction settings. Models that score health risk or creditworthiness can improve service allocation, but underlying histories are sensitive. Without minimization, access control, and purpose limits, the same features that raise utility become levers for harm. Open the example 7 module for this chapter to review the tension checklist.

Slide 5 — Ethical dilemmas in practice

Dilemmas arise when principles conflict or no option is clearly harm-free. Example 3.8 sketches criminal-justice risk scores: tools can appear useful for scheduling yet reproduce racial disparities in historical data—accuracy for the institution conflicts with fairness for affected communities. Example 3.9 returns to platform collection: fine-grained logging may enrich recommendations users never meaningfully agreed to share.

Slide 6 — Example 3.8 — Dilemma in criminal justice risk scores

Example 3.8 is the justice-system conflict previewed again in the bias section and Chapter 7. More data alone does not dissolve the conflict; teams must ask who bears residual risk and whether limits can be monitored after launch. Try the example 8 module to compare institutional metrics with community harm.

Slide 7 — Consequences of neglecting ethics

Ignoring ethics produces privacy violations, discrimination, eroded trust, and reinforced inequality. Example 3.10 shows personal exposure after unauthorized use or leakage—fraud, harassment, and lasting harm for individuals, plus trust loss across the wider user base.

Slide 8 — Example 3.10 — Misuse of personal data

Example 3.10 connects ethical neglect to breach economics developed later in the chapter. Organizations also face fines, litigation, and reputational damage under regimes such as the GDPR. Ethical practice is both a moral and a sustainability requirement.

Slide 9 — Takeaways

Utility and privacy trade off by design—not by accident. Dilemmas need structured decision procedures, not silent defaults. Neglecting ethics harms people first and balance sheets second.
