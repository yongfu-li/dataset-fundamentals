By the end of this part, you should describe AI-scale privacy risks, name two global regulation friction patterns, and identify near-term directions including privacy-enhancing technologies and stronger individual control models.

## Slide 3 — AI and privacy concerns

Large-scale sensing and prediction create surveillance capacity that traditional notice-and-consent models struggle to govern. Example 3.35 focuses on facial analysis at city scale: commercial systems have shown large accuracy gaps across phenotype and gender subgroups, turning deployments into both privacy and discrimination risks when used for identification or screening.

## Slide 4 — Example 3.35 — Surveillance and facial analysis

Example 3.35 connects technical accuracy gaps to civil-liberties risk. Open the example 35 module for this chapter to review subgroup error in vision systems. Example 3.36 extends the pattern to leakage from sensitive predictive models in healthcare—outputs and embeddings can expose patients if releases are poorly de-identified.

## Slide 5 — Ethics debates in deployed AI

Open debates continue over how far deployers must go beyond average accuracy, who is accountable when automated systems harm people, and how much human control must remain in high-stakes loops. Example 3.37 illustrates autonomous vehicle liability: responsibility maps to manufacturers, operators, and governance regimes—not to the model as a moral agent.

## Slide 6 — Global privacy regulation challenges

Cross-border products confront overlapping rules rather than a single global code. Example 3.38 shows a multinational needing GDPR posture for EU residents while honoring CCPA and CPRA rights for California residents—parallel notice, retention, and request-handling workflows. Localization mandates and transfer restrictions, as in Examples 3.39 and 3.40, can force regional infrastructure when a single global cloud would be simpler operationally.

## Slide 7 — Example 3.38 — Conflicting privacy regimes

Example 3.38 is the operational cost of running multiple statutory programs in one product. Try the example 38 module to map jurisdiction-specific workflows. Schrems II and similar adequacy failures tighten expectations for cross-border transfers teams must anticipate in architecture.

## Slide 8 — Future directions

Near-term practice will keep combining privacy-enhancing technologies from Section 3.4, clearer AI governance, stronger individual control models, and Privacy by Design defaults. Example 3.41 sketches self-sovereign identity proposals that let people hold and present credentials without a single intermediary owning the identity graph.

## Slide 9 — Example 3.41 — Self-sovereign identity

Example 3.41 aligns with stronger individual control over personal data. Open the example 41 module for this chapter to review credential models. The open question is adoption speed—not whether the building blocks exist.
