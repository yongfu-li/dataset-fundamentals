## Slide 1 — Chapter 3 — Introduction to ethics and privacy

Chapter 2 asked how records enter a dataset. This chapter asks what obligations attach once those records concern people. This opening part defines ethics and privacy, shows how they reinforce each other, and frames ethical data work as a lifecycle-wide coordination problem.

## Slide 2 — Learning objectives

By the end of this part, you should distinguish ethics from privacy, name stakeholders affected across the dataset lifecycle, and explain why compliance alone does not guarantee ethical practice.

## Slide 3 — Why ethics and privacy matter now

Data-driven systems shape decisions in healthcare, finance, marketing, and government. As collection and use scale, teams face a duty to handle personal information transparently, fairly, and accountably. Misuse can enable identity theft, fraud, discrimination, and loss of autonomy—even without a headline breach, opaque practices erode trust.

## Slide 4 — Defining ethics and privacy

In data work, ethics refers to principles that guide how data are collected, used, shared, and governed—fairness, transparency, accountability, and non-maleficence. Privacy denotes individuals' rights to control personal data: to consent, to know what is collected and why, and often to access, correct, or delete records. Ethics asks whether a use should proceed; privacy constrains how permitted uses are implemented.

## Slide 5 — Ethics versus privacy at a glance

Ethics focuses on who is harmed or helped and whether uses are fair and accountable. Privacy focuses on meaningful consent and individual control. A team can be legally compliant yet ethically weak if uses are opaque or discriminatory; a privacy notice alone does not make a pipeline ethical if foreseeable harms are ignored.

## Slide 6 — Lifecycle and stakeholders

Ethics binds every stage—not only model release but collection, annotation, cleaning, exploration, and documentation. Stakeholders include practitioners, leaders, policymakers, and centrally data subjects whose records are at stake. No single role owns the full obligation.

## Slide 7 — Example 3.1 — Stakeholders in a wellness app launch

Example 3.1 compresses those roles into one product decision. Product wants finer activity tracking; legal wants clearer consent; executives want partner sharing; users want control over heart-rate history. Ethical design must negotiate utility, consent, and minimization together. Open the example 1 module for this chapter to review the stakeholder map.

## Slide 8 — Takeaways

Remember three points. Ethics and privacy are distinct but mutually reinforcing. Obligations span the full dataset lifecycle, not only deployment. Ethical data work is coordinated across roles—utility, consent, and revenue pressure collide in ordinary feature decisions.
