## Slide 1 — Chapter 7 — Impacts of dataset bias

Bias detection is not merely a technical quality exercise. Disparities can change access to employment, credit, healthcare, liberty, and public services. This clip organizes those consequences into ethical, social, legal, and business dimensions while showing how one high-stakes system can create several forms of harm at once.

## Slide 2 — Learning objectives

By the end of this clip, learners should distinguish four impact dimensions, trace how biased data can amplify existing inequities, explain why intent is not required for harmful impact, and identify the subgroup evidence and accountability records needed for high-stakes deployment.

## Slide 3 — Example 7.38 — Inequitable Treatment in Credit and Justice Decisions

Example 7.38 illustrates the ethical impact of unequal treatment. Biased credit data may deny loans for demographic reasons rather than genuine creditworthiness, while biased justice data may produce unfair risk assessments. Such errors affect opportunities and liberty, so fairness cannot be treated as optional polish added after average accuracy is maximized.

## Slide 4 — Example 7.40 — Amplification of Policing and Hiring Disparities

Example 7.40 shows social amplification. Historical over-policing can generate more recorded incidents in already monitored communities, which then directs still more policing there. Historical hiring patterns can similarly teach a ranking system to favor men. Repeated automated decisions scale and reinforce the pattern, creating a feedback loop that appears data-driven.

## Slide 5 — Example 7.42 — Compliance Exposure Under GDPR and CCPA

Example 7.42 connects unfair outcomes with legal and regulatory exposure. Recruitment and credit systems must comply with anti-discrimination duties, while privacy frameworks require fair and transparent processing of personal data. Organizations need more than a claim of neutral intent; they need documented data provenance, subgroup testing, explanations, and corrective procedures.

## Slide 6 — Example 7.45 — Financial Penalties from Biased Hiring and Credit Systems

Example 7.45 presents the business consequences. Biased systems can generate fines, lawsuits, project cancellations, remediation costs, customer loss, and lasting reputational damage. Fairness investment can therefore reduce risk and protect market legitimacy. Delayed mitigation is often more expensive because deployed decisions and public harms are difficult to reverse.

## Slide 7 — The COMPAS bridge

Recidivism risk assessment provides a bridge across all four dimensions. Unequal error rates can change detention or parole exposure, reproduce historical arrest disparities, raise due-process and discrimination concerns, and weaken institutional legitimacy. Overall accuracy does not settle these issues because false positives and false negatives carry different consequences for different groups.

## Slide 8 — From impact to accountability

High-stakes systems require documented subgroup error rates, known limitations, decision owners, appeal mechanisms, and monitoring after deployment. Impact assessments should ask who can be harmed, how severe and reversible the harm is, and whether a less risky alternative exists. These questions connect technical metrics to organizational responsibility.

## Slide 9 — Takeaways

Ethical impacts concern justice and individual treatment. Social impacts concern stereotypes, feedback loops, and unequal access. Legal impacts concern rights, discrimination, privacy, and explainability. Business impacts include cost, reputation, and legitimacy. A single disparity can cross all four dimensions, especially in consequential decisions.
