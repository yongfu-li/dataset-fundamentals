Slide 1 — Chapter 9 — Crowdsourcing use cases

When human judgment must scale beyond a small expert team, crowdsourcing distributes work to a large online community. This part defines crowdsourcing, outlines common task types, and shows use cases from fraud-related review and innovation contests to microtask image tagging.

Slide 2 — Learning objectives

By the end of this part, learners should be able to define crowdsourcing and name major task types; describe labeling, market research, innovation, and microtasking use cases; and interpret examples of fraud-pattern review, open algorithmic contests, and catalog image tagging.

Slide 3 — What crowdsourcing is

Crowdsourcing obtains input, services, or resources by soliciting contributions from a large group, typically through an online community. It draws on collective intelligence and diverse skills for problems that are too complex or time-consuming for a small team alone. The approach can lower cost while also tapping creativity, not only routine labor.

Slide 4 — Types of crowdsourcing tasks

Common types include crowd voting on ideas or designs; crowd creation of writing, design, or code; crowd collaboration on complex research or software problems; crowdfunding for projects; and crowd labor that splits work into microtasks such as labeling, transcription, or categorization. Dataset projects most often emphasize crowd labor and related labeling workflows.

Slide 5 — Labeling, research, and fraud review

Machine learning teams use crowdsourcing to gather labeled images and text quickly. Market research can sample broad demographics through surveys on the same platforms. Example settings also apply crowd-scale review patterns to large transaction corpora when fraud risk must be assessed from amount, location, and frequency signals.

Slide 6 — Example 9.8 — Crowdsourcing Fraud-Pattern Review

Example 9.8 describes a bank analyzing millions of transactions for fraud likelihood based on amounts, locations, and frequency. Applied in near real time, such a model can flag and help prevent fraud before customers are harmed. Open the example 8 module for this chapter to review the fraud-review framing.

Slide 7 — Example 9.9 — Netflix Prize Crowdsourced Innovation

Example 9.9 recalls the Netflix Prize: a one-million-dollar open competition for the best algorithm to improve recommendations. Organizations use contests to crowdsource innovation when many external teams can explore solutions under a shared goal. The example 9 module for this chapter summarizes that contest-style innovation pattern.

Slide 8 — Example 9.10 — Microtask Image Tagging on Crowd Platforms

Example 9.10 shows catalog image tagging split into microtasks—for example labeling apparel, electronics, or home goods—and distributed to many workers. Thousands of items can be annotated in hours rather than weeks. The example 10 module for this chapter captures that microtasking speed advantage.

Slide 9 — Takeaways

Crowdsourcing scales human judgment through online communities and multiple task types. Dataset work often centers on labeling and microtasking, while contests can crowdsource innovation. Fraud review, open prizes, and catalog tagging illustrate how the same idea adapts to different collection goals.
