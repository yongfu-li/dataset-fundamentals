## Slide 1 — Chapter 6 — Dispersion, shape, and grouping

A mean without spread is ambiguous. This part covers variance, standard deviation, range, distribution shape, frequency tables, and group-by aggregation.

## Slide 2 — Learning objectives

By the end of this part, you should compute variance and standard deviation, interpret range, read skewness cues, build frequency distributions, and summarize by groups.

## Slide 3 — Variance and standard deviation

Variance averages squared deviations from the mean; standard deviation is its square root in the original units. Example 6.18 sketches variance for a small sample; Example 6.19 takes the square root. Larger standard deviation means more spread around the center.

## Slide 4 — Example 6.18 — Variance sketch

Example 6.18 uses the sample values two, four, four, six, and eight. Open the example 18 module for this chapter to follow the deviation steps. Standard deviation complements mean in describe() output.

## Slide 5 — Range

Range is max minus min—Example 6.20 on a small sample. Simple but sensitive to outliers. Pair range with quartiles or IQR for robust spread views in later plots.

## Slide 6 — Measures of shape

Skewness indicates asymmetry: right-skewed income tails pull the mean above the median. Kurtosis relates to tail heaviness. Example 6.2 from the introduction linked skewness to model choice—log transforms or robust methods may follow.

## Slide 7 — Frequency distributions and grouping

Frequency tables count category occurrences or bin counts for numerics. Group-by summaries compute mean spend by region or category—Example 6.9 illustrates geography and purchase frequency hypotheses. Aggregation reveals segment differences raw global means hide.

## Slide 8 — Takeaways

Report center and spread together. Shape guides transforms and model families. Frequency and group-by tables bridge summaries to the visual techniques in the next parts.
